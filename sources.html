Sources & Methodology

This tool provides material-specific environmental risk assessment based on established preservation science research. Below you'll find our sources, methodology, and important limitations.

Psychrometric Calculations

Magnus Formula

Dew point calculations use the Magnus formula for saturation vapor pressure:

SVP = 610.78 × exp((17.27 × T) / (237.7 + T))

Where T is temperature in Celsius and SVP is in Pascals. This formula is accurate within ±0.4% for temperatures between -40°C and 50°C.

Absolute Humidity

Calculated using the ideal gas law: AH = (vapor pressure × 18.016) / (8.314 × TK)

Where 18.016 g/mol is the molecular weight of water and 8.314 J/(mol·K) is the gas constant.

Primary Sources by Material Type

General Collections

  • ASHRAE Applications Handbook (2019) - Museums, Galleries, Archives and Libraries chapter
  • NEDCC Preservation Leaflets - 2.1 Temperature, Relative Humidity, Light, and Air Quality
  • Bizot Green Protocol (2023) - Environmental guidelines balancing preservation and sustainability

Recommended: 16-25°C (60.8-77°F), 40-60% RH with ±10% daily fluctuation for general hygroscopic materials

Paper & Manuscripts

  • Library of Congress - Care, Handling and Storage of Works on Paper
  • NISO TR01 - Environmental Guidelines for the Storage of Paper Records (2001)
  • Smithsonian Institution Archives - Collections Care Guidelines

Optimal: 65-70°F, 30-50% RH. RH control crucial as moisture catalyzes acid formation in paper.

Photographs

  • Library of Congress - Information Leaflet on Care, Handling, and Storage of Photographs
  • Image Permanence Institute (IPI) - Cold Storage Guidelines and Preservation Metrics
  • NPS Conserve O Gram 14/10 - Cold Storage for Photograph Collections
  • ISO Standards - ISO 18911:2010 for imaging materials

B&W Photos: 60-68°F (extended-term ≤64°F), 30-40% RH
Color Photos: 35-40°F (cold storage required), 30-40% RH. Chromogenic dyes deteriorate rapidly above 65°F.

Textiles & Fabrics

  • Smithsonian Museum Conservation Institute - Climate and Textiles Storage
  • Canadian Conservation Institute - CCI Notes on Textiles and the Environment
  • George Washington University Textile Museum - Conservation guidelines

Optimal: 65-70°F, 45-55% RH. Stability essential - RH fluctuations cause fiber expansion/contraction leading to damage.

Leather & Parchment

  • NPS Appendix S - Curatorial Care of Objects Made From Leather and Skin Products
  • Canadian Conservation Institute - Caring for Leather, Skin and Fur
  • Museum Guidelines (Australia) - Environmental conditions for museum collections

Optimal: 64-68°F, 45-55% RH. Critical thresholds: <35% RH causes desiccation and cracking, >65% RH promotes mold and "red rot".

Metal Objects

  • Canadian Conservation Institute - CCI Notes 9/2: Storage of Metals
  • NPS Metal Objects Guide - Preservation of historic metal artifacts
  • Museum Australia - Metals Collections Care

General metals: 60-68°F, 30-50% RH (≤55% max)
Sensitive metals (iron, bronze): 60-68°F, 12-35% RH. Iron most stable at ≤12% RH. Contaminated iron requires <35% RH.

Magnetic Tape & Optical Media

  • Library of Congress - Care, Handling, and Storage of Audio Visual Materials
  • NEDCC Session 6 - Storage and Handling of Media Collections
  • CLIR - Magnetic Tape Storage and Handling Guide
  • ISO 9660 - Standard for optical media storage

Magnetic Tape: 50-65°F (permanent value: 46-50°F), 30-40% RH. Higher humidity causes binder hydrolysis.
Optical Media: 64-70°F, 30-50% RH. Avoid thermal cycling.

Wood & Furniture

  • Canadian Conservation Institute - Wood preservation guidelines
  • General Museum Guidelines - Environmental conditions for wooden objects

Optimal: 65-70°F, 45-55% RH. Wood is highly hygroscopic - RH fluctuations cause warping, cracking, and joint failure.

Fluctuation Thresholds

Temperature Fluctuation:

  • General collections: ±4-5°F per 24 hours acceptable
  • Sensitive materials (photographs, paper): ±2-3°F recommended

Relative Humidity Fluctuation:

  • Bizot Green Protocol (2023): ±10% per 24 hours for general collections
  • Sensitive materials (paper, photographs, textiles): ±5% or less
  • Very sensitive materials (leather, wood): ±3-4% recommended

Source: Bizot Green Protocol (2023), IIC/ICOM-CC Declaration (2014), ASHRAE Museum Guidelines

Decay Risk Methodology

Risk assessments are based on four decay mechanisms established by preservation research:

1. Natural Aging

Chemical deterioration driven primarily by temperature. Every 10°F increase approximately doubles the rate of chemical reactions (Arrhenius equation). Lower temperatures slow degradation for all organic materials.

2. Mechanical Decay

Physical damage from dimensional changes as materials expand/contract with RH fluctuations. Hygroscopic materials (paper, wood, textiles, leather) are most affected. Optimal RH ranges minimize stress.

3. Mold Growth

Fungal growth risk based on combined temperature and RH. Mold spores begin germinating above 65% RH, with risk increasing significantly at 70% RH combined with warm temperatures (70°F+). Organic materials are most susceptible.

Source: EPA Mold Prevention Guidelines, PMC Study on Temperature vs RH for Mold Prevention

4. Metal Corrosion

Oxidation and corrosion driven by moisture availability (dew point) and RH. Condensation at dew point dramatically accelerates metal corrosion. Different metals have specific RH thresholds below which corrosion is minimal.

Important Limitations

⚠️ This Tool Does NOT Replace Professional Assessment

  • Simplified Risk Model: Real preservation risk depends on many factors beyond temperature and humidity, including: object condition, material composition, history, pollutants, light exposure, and handling practices.
  • General Guidelines: Risk ratings are based on general material categories. Individual objects may have specific requirements based on their condition, provenance, or composition.
  • Static Measurements: Single-point measurements don't capture trends, seasonal variations, or cyclical patterns. Continuous monitoring is essential for proper environmental management.
  • No Air Quality Assessment: This tool doesn't account for pollutants, particulates, or air quality factors that significantly impact preservation.
  • Fluctuation Estimates: Fluctuation analysis requires user-estimated values and doesn't replace continuous environmental monitoring systems.
  • Sustainability Trade-offs: Optimal preservation conditions must be balanced against institutional sustainability goals and energy constraints. See Bizot Green Protocol (2023) for guidance on balancing preservation with environmental responsibility.

Best Practices for Use

  • Use as a screening tool to identify potential environmental concerns
  • Implement continuous monitoring using dataloggers or environmental monitoring systems
  • Consult with conservators for collection-specific guidance and treatment decisions
  • Consider material sensitivity when selecting collection types - choose the most sensitive material in mixed collections
  • Document conditions over time to identify trends and seasonal patterns
  • Balance preservation with sustainability using frameworks like the Bizot Green Protocol
